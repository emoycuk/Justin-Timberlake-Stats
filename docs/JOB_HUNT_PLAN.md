# Job Hunt Plan — 5 Cold Email Targets

This is the prioritized list. Send in order. **One per week max** — quality over quantity. If a company doesn't respond in 2 weeks, follow up *once*, then move on.

---

## Before you send anything

Do these once. They take 1-2 hours total and apply to every email.

1. **Custom domain live** on the site (jtstats.com or similar). Vercel link in an email = ignored.
2. **Case study published** on Medium with your real name + photo. (See `CASE_STUDY_OUTLINE.md`.)
3. **LinkedIn updated:**
   - Headline: "Independent Music Data Analyst · Building Timberlake Analytics"
   - Featured section: pinned link to the site + the case study
   - About: 2 paragraphs explaining what you do, ending with what you're looking for
   - Activity: at least 3 posts in the past month sharing music data insights
4. **GitHub repo polished:** README live, pinned on your profile, profile photo matches LinkedIn
5. **One pinned tweet** linking to the case study

You want a recruiter who Googles "Emir Kilic" to see a coherent person across LinkedIn, GitHub, X, and Medium — all pointing to the same project. This takes a weekend.

## The 5 targets (ranked)

### #1 — Chartmetric 🟢 (Toronto, hybrid/remote-friendly)

**Why first:** Their entire product is essentially what you built, but scaled to every artist. Founder Sung Cho is hands-on and replies to good DMs. They actively hire interns and junior analysts from non-traditional backgrounds.

**Who to reach:**
- **Sung Cho** — Founder/CEO, very accessible on LinkedIn, occasionally on X
- **Head of Data** (check current LinkedIn — turnover happens)
- Posted roles: check `chartmetric.com/careers` weekly

**Angle:** "I built a single-artist version of what you ship at scale. Here's what I learned about catalog-level analytics that I think generalizes."

---

### #2 — Spotify (Istanbul, Stockholm, London, NYC) 🟢

**Why second:** Has an Istanbul office (good if you're in Turkey). Has an Editorial team that hires music-obsessed analysts. Has a Data Science org that hires engineers. Many entry points.

**Who to reach:**
- **Spotify Istanbul** — find current Spotify Türkiye employees on LinkedIn, ask for a 15-min coffee chat (don't ask for a job, ask about their work)
- **Editorial team leads** — they hire data-savvy curators
- **University Programs / Internship coordinator** — Spotify has a structured internship program; apply through `lifeatspotify.com`

**Angle:** Different per team. For Editorial: "I have deep knowledge of one catalog and the data tools to back it up." For Data Science: "I've built a real production pipeline against your public API and Kworb, here's the architecture."

**Reality:** Spotify is a marquee employer, gets thousands of applicants. Your site moves you from "rejection email" to "phone screen." It does not guarantee an offer.

---

### #3 — Luminate (formerly MRC Data / Nielsen Music) 🟡

**Why third:** They are the data source behind Billboard charts. Less well-known than Spotify but enormous in the music industry. Hire data analysts and engineers heavily. Remote-friendly for some roles.

**Who to reach:**
- **VP of Music Insights** — find current on LinkedIn
- **Data engineering managers** — search "Luminate data engineer" on LinkedIn, look at their teams
- Posted roles: `luminatedata.com/careers`

**Angle:** "I built a single-artist analytics platform using your kind of methodology. I'd love to learn how you handle this at industry scale."

---

### #4 — Soundcharts (Paris, sometimes remote) 🟢

**Why fourth:** European competitor to Chartmetric. Smaller team → individual contributors get noticed. They specifically hire people who understand artist-level data.

**Who to reach:**
- **David Weiszfeld** — CEO, active on LinkedIn
- Their team is small (~30-50 people); easy to figure out who runs data

**Angle:** Similar to Chartmetric. Lead with the site, mention you've thought about how their dataset and yours would intersect.

---

### #5 — A music journalism outlet's data desk 🟡

Pick **one** of:
- **Billboard** (chart team, data journalism)
- **Hits Daily Double** (very specific, niche, scrappy)
- **MusicAlly** (UK-based music business analytics newsletter)
- **Variety** (data-driven music coverage)

**Why fifth:** Different career track, but if you like writing as much as building, this is a real path. Music data journalists are rare and valuable.

**Who to reach:** Find a reporter who recently wrote a data-driven piece on a similar artist. Email them: "I built a tool that does X. Would love to be a source the next time you need a number for an article."

**Angle:** Not a job application — you're offering to be a reference / source. If they use you once and you're good, the relationship grows.

## The cold email template

Use this exactly. Subject line and length matter.

```
Subject: Built a live analytics platform for one artist's catalog — would love your take

Hi [First name],

I'm Emir, an independent music data analyst. I built [jtstats.com] — a
live analytics platform tracking Justin Timberlake's entire solo catalog:
Spotify streams recomputed on every page load, 60+ country certifications
with per-country thresholds, EAS using Chartmasters CSPC methodology, and
year-end projection from weighted growth blends.

I'm interested in [Chartmetric / Spotify / etc.] because [one specific
reason — name a product feature, a recent blog post they published, or a
team member's talk you watched]. The hardest part of building this was
[one specific technical challenge, 1 sentence — e.g., reconciling track
title spellings across four data sources], and I think a lot about how
this would scale beyond one artist.

I'd love 15 minutes to hear how your team thinks about [their core
problem — e.g., catalog ingestion, artist-level analytics, label
relationships]. Not asking for a job — just want to learn from people
who do this professionally.

Case study: [medium link]
Code: [github link]

Best,
Emir Kilic
[X handle] · [LinkedIn]
```

### Rules
- **Under 200 words.** They will not read more.
- **No attachments.** Links only.
- **One specific reason per company.** Generic "I love your product" gets deleted.
- **End with a 15-min ask, not a job ask.** Lower friction → higher response rate.
- **Send Tuesday–Thursday, 10am–11am their local time.** Best open rates.

---

## What to do when they reply

**If they say yes to a call:**
- Prepare 3 specific questions about *their* work, not yours
- Have your site open in one tab to demo if they ask
- End with: "What would make me a strong candidate when you next hire?"
- Send a thank-you email within 24 hours

**If they say "we're not hiring right now":**
- Reply: "Totally understand — would it be okay if I stayed in touch and shared updates as the project grows?"
- Most will say yes. Now you have a warm contact for 6 months later.

**If they ghost:**
- One follow-up after 7-10 days. Two sentences max.
- After that: move on. No third follow-up.

## Realistic expectations

- **Response rate on cold emails to non-recruiters: 15-25%** if the email is good and the recipient is reachable. Lower if you batch-send the same template.
- **Of responders, ~30% will agree to a 15-min call.** So 5 emails → ~1 call expected.
- **Calls that convert to interviews: ~20-30%.** So you need 10-15 calls to land 3-4 real interview processes.
- **Time horizon: 3-6 months** from "first email" to "offer" is normal for music data roles. Plan accordingly.

Don't get demoralized by silence. The math says this works if you keep going. Most people quit at email 3-4.

## One more thing

The single most underrated career move is **publishing publicly while you do this.** Post on X every 1-2 weeks: "JT just crossed X streams" or "interesting thing I noticed in the certifications data." Tag music data accounts (@chartdata, @PopBase, @ChartMetric). One viral-ish post gets you more attention than 20 cold emails.

The site is your work. The posts make people find you.
